Subnetting Review

Consider Phone Numbers:

(416) 872-1973 1

Subnetting Review

416
Area Code. Tells about the region.
872-1973
The rest of the digits. In this area we have 10 Million possible numbers.

Subnetting Review II

…It’s the same with Subnetting. The left side of our IP address gives us a general identifier…

192.168.0.100

Subnetting Review II

…while the right side gives us a more specific address inside our ‘area’.

192.168.0.100

Where we separate these two parts is defined by the mask (where is it here?).

Subnetting Review III

Let’s simplify the idea with an “address” of only 8 bits, rather than the 32 bits we usually use.

1 1 1 1 1 1 1 1
127 64 32 16 8 4 2 1

Subnetting Review III

/4 means we are masking our 4 most significant bits…

1111 1111

Subnetting Review III

…leaving us with 4 bits as available ‘space’ in our area, or subnet.

1 1 1 1
8 4 2 1

Subnetting Review III

However, 2 of those addresses are reserved: the network address and the broadcast address.

  • 0 reserved, network address
  • 1 - 14 ok
  • 15 reserved, broadcast address

Sample Subnet Question

Let’s say that you are instructed to set up a VPC and a subnet inside of it. The VPC is expected to hold no more than 130 possible instances, and the subnet no more than 66. Create the smallest possible subnet and VPC that you can.

  1. What will be the CIDR of the VPC?

  2. What will be the CIDR of the subnet?

  3. How many total usable addresses will the subnet have? For this exercise, exclude the network address.

  4. What is the range of usable addresses in the subnet?

Sample Subnet Question

  • VPC holds no more than 130 instances, what’s the smallest number of bits that will hold that number?
1 1 1 1 1 1 1 1
127 64 32 16 8 4 2 1

Answer

0111 1111
This equals 127, not enough!
1111 1111
This equals 255, this is our answer! We need 8 bits to hold the addresses of 130 instances.

Since it isn’t specified, we can use 10.3.45.0/24.

32 bits total - 8 bits = 24 bits.

Answer II

Within that VPC, we are going to make a further division and create a smaller subnet, which will use a portion of those 255 possible addresses. No more than 66.

What’s the smallest number of bits that will hold that number?

1 1 1 1 1 1 1 1
127 64 32 16 8 4 2 1

Answer II

0111 1111 = 127, again significantly more than 66 but it’s important not to run out!

32 bits - 7 bits = 25. Our CIDR is 10.3.45/25.

Answer III

127 - 2 = 125

Address range is this:

000 0000 to 111 1111

In Decimal:

0 to 127. Except that the very top and very bottom of that range aren’t usable! So:

10.3.45.1 to 10.3.45.126.
